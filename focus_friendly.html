A diode is a two-terminal semiconductor device that allows electric current to flow in only one direction, functioning as a one-way switch for current. It consists of a PN junction, where the P-type material contains holes and the N-type material contains electrons. When the diode is forward biased (positive to P, negative to N), it conducts electricity after crossing a certain threshold voltage (typically 0.7V for silicon). In reverse bias (positive to N, negative to P), it blocks current, acting as an insulator. Diodes are widely used for rectification (converting AC to DC), signal demodulation, voltage regulation (Zener diodes), light emission (LEDs), and overvoltage protection. Special types include Schottky diodes for fast switching, photodiodes for light sensing, and varactor diodes for tuning circuits.
